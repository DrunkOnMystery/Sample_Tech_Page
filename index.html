The Early Days of Morolith

The world of Morolith was once a place of mighty civilizations and powerful gods. For centuries, the many gods of that world walked the plains and forests alongside the mighty civilizations of wizards and warriors that populated the world’s four large continents and the surrounding island masses. Mighty empires, filled with powerful mages and wondrous technological achievements sprouted all over the world. Life in that lost age was one of unspeakable wealth and comfort.

The Great Exile

But nothing lasts forever, and eventually both the civilizations and the gods themselves fell to in-fighting. Entire cities and kingdoms fell to dust, as the fighting spread across the world. Gods fought each other, and made mortals into their champions, shaping them into tools of their wills. When finally, the dozens of gods had laid waste to entire continents, and gods themselves had begun to be killed by their brethren, that the remaining gods, realizing the potential destruction of the whole of Morolith was imminent, agreed to a plan you know of now as “The Exile”.

The few gods whose power had not been greatly diminished by the constant fighting opened the veil between this world and the next, and forced every god they could through it, closing it behind them. Only a few gods, desperately weakened by the fighting, remained in Morolith, and in their weakened states, they could not defend themselves, even from the mortals that remained, and were forced to hide in fear. And once the other gods crossed the veil, they could no longer directly intervene in the lives of the mortals of Morolith. Instead, they could only lend their aid and boons indirectly and magically to those they chose.

The Dark Age

In the centuries after the Exile, the great kingdoms of Morolith all collapsed. Where gods had fallen in death, the lands themselves were permanently altered: fertile farmlands became desolate wastelands and vice versa, seemingly overnight. Men fought over changed landscapes and dwindling resources. Over the next 500 years, the population of Morolith declined by nearly 90%. Kingdoms rose and fell on every continent constantly.

The Rise of Valbaan

But slowly but surely, the races recovered, and new kingdoms, these far longer lasting, began to arise. On the western continent of Lisanthus, one such kingdom was the kingdom of Valbaan. Led by its king, Valbus I, often known as Valbus the Wise or Valbus the Just, the Kingdom of Valbaan rose from its status as a small kingdom in the Eastern plains to be an empire larger than any other kingdom on Lisanthus.

For more than three centuries, the kings and queens of Valbaan ruled an ever-expanding kingdom, and its many innovations, discoveries, and cultural advancements and reforms were influential, spreading far and wide throughout Lisanthus. For many years, it could be safely said that Valbaan was the envy of all of Lisanthus, if not all the world.

The Death of King Jandar

But in the year 847 AE, disaster struck Valbaan: a plague struck the capital city, and it claimed the lives of many men, women, and children. And chief among the dead was the young King Jandar II, the last direct descendent of the line of Valbus I. With the king dead, and with him having no heir, the throne fell to a distant cousin of Jandar’s named Mychel, a fool who fancied himself wise and a coward who fancied himself brave.

The Rise of the Twelve Sages

Quickly, a group of advisers and nobles within the king’s court, referred to, mostly sarcastically, by the citizenry as “the Twelve Sages”, had taken full control of the capital and government, manipulating King Mychel to their advantage, all the while allowing the foolish king to indulge his every whim while his court seized wealth and power for themselves, ignoring the citizenry throughout the kingdom all the while. Neighboring kingdoms began incursions across the border, claiming Valbaanese territory for themselves. The borders of Valbaan began shrinking, monster raids became a far more frequent problem, the workers and armies of the kingdom were often paid late if they were paid at all, and discontent mounted amongst the population across the kingdom, with only the provincial governments serving as any kind of aid for the set-upon citizenry.

The Rebellion of the Beranthians

Finally, in the year 861 AE, a full-scale rebellion, led by Zuran, the leader of a new cult worshipping the ancient, exiled god Beranthus, broke out in the northern provinces, attracting thousands to its cause. The “unfaithful” were slaughtered, towns were destroyed, and thousands of refugees were sent fleeing South for their lives. The Royal Army, poorly led, underpaid, and given frequently confused and contradictory orders, was scattered before the Beranthians when they clashed at the Battle of Melitas River in 862 AE, and, with the Royal Army seemingly outmatched and no aid coming, many more still joined Zuran’s cause.

Fearing the capital itself might eventually be overrun, the king and the Twelve Sages sent out a call asking every provincial governor, lord, and bannerman of the king to raise soldiers and march them to the capital to create a singular Grand Army to put down the rebellion. Though there was some resistance from various lords and governors, soon the Grand Army was formed and, after a series of smaller battles throughout the kingdom of Valbaan, they finally defeated the Beranthian Rebellion once and for all at the Battle of the Red Mountain in 864 AE.
